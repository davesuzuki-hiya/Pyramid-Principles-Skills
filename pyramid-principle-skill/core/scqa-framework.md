# SCQA Framework

The SCQA (Situation-Complication-Question-Answer) framework is the foundation of structured communication. It creates a narrative arc that captures attention and guides the audience to your conclusion.

## The Four Elements

### Situation (S)
The stable, undisputed context that everyone agrees on.

**Characteristics:**
- Factual and verifiable
- Sets time and place
- Establishes common ground
- Should not be controversial

**Example:**
> "Hiya's PLG product line has grown to represent 40% of MRR over the past 18 months."

### Complication (C)
The change, tension, or problem that disrupts the stable situation.

**Characteristics:**
- Creates urgency
- Explains why action is needed NOW
- Often involves a threat or opportunity
- The "so what" that demands attention

**Example:**
> "However, conversion rates from free trial to paid have declined 23% quarter-over-quarter, and CAC payback has extended from 8 to 14 months."

### Question (Q)
The specific question that arises naturally from the complication.

**Characteristics:**
- Directly addresses the complication
- Actionable and answerable
- Often begins with "How can we..." or "What should we..."
- The question your audience is now asking themselves

**Example:**
> "How can we restore conversion efficiency while maintaining growth velocity?"

### Answer (A)
Your recommendation or conclusion—stated upfront.

**Characteristics:**
- Direct and actionable
- Supported by the analysis that follows
- Should be testable/provable
- The "governing thought" of your pyramid

**Example:**
> "We recommend a three-pronged approach: (1) implement value-based onboarding segmentation, (2) introduce a mid-tier pricing option, and (3) add usage-based triggers for sales engagement."

## Variations by Context

### SCQA (Standard)
Best for: Recommendations, proposals, strategy documents
Flow: Situation → Complication → Question → Answer

### SCR (Situation-Complication-Resolution)
Best for: Executive summaries, quick updates
Flow: Situation → Complication → Resolution (skips explicit Question)

### QSCA (Question-first)
Best for: Research presentations, when audience knows the question
Flow: Question → Situation → Complication → Answer

## Real-World Examples

### Example 1: PLG Strategy Pivot

**Situation:**
Hiya launched its consumer app in 2022 with a freemium model, achieving 2M downloads in the first year.

**Complication:**
Despite high download volume, only 0.8% of users convert to paid plans—significantly below the 3-5% industry benchmark for security apps. User interviews reveal that most users don't understand the premium value proposition until after they've experienced a spam call that the free tier couldn't block.

**Question:**
How can we increase conversion rates by helping users experience premium value earlier in their journey?

**Answer:**
Implement a "Smart Trial" feature that automatically activates premium blocking for the first 10 suspicious calls, demonstrating value before requiring payment. Projected impact: 2.4% conversion rate (+200% improvement).

### Example 2: Market Entry Decision

**Situation:**
Company X dominates the US market with 45% share. The board has allocated $50M for international expansion.

**Complication:**
Three markets show opportunity (UK, Germany, Japan), but each requires different go-to-market strategies, regulatory compliance, and localization investment. We cannot pursue all three simultaneously with current resources.

**Question:**
Which market should we enter first to maximize ROI and establish a beachhead for further European/Asian expansion?

**Answer:**
Enter the UK market first. Rationale: (1) Lowest localization cost (shared language), (2) Regulatory framework most similar to US, (3) Can serve as EU testing ground before broader expansion.

## Common Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Situation too long
**Problem:** Spending 3 slides on background before getting to the point.
**Fix:** Situation should be 1-2 sentences. If audience needs more background, put it in an appendix.

### Mistake 2: Complication is vague
**Problem:** "Things are changing" or "We face challenges"
**Fix:** Quantify the complication. Use specific metrics, dates, and impacts.

### Mistake 3: Question doesn't follow naturally
**Problem:** Question feels forced or disconnected from complication.
**Fix:** The question should be what your audience is already thinking after hearing the complication.

### Mistake 4: Answer is too hedge-y
**Problem:** "We should consider evaluating options for potentially..."
**Fix:** Be direct. "We recommend X." You can add caveats later, but lead with clarity.

## SCQA Quality Checklist

- [ ] Situation is factual and undisputed
- [ ] Complication creates clear urgency
- [ ] Complication is quantified where possible
- [ ] Question flows naturally from S+C
- [ ] Question is specific and actionable
- [ ] Answer directly addresses the question
- [ ] Answer is stated as a clear recommendation
- [ ] Total SCQA can be delivered in <60 seconds
